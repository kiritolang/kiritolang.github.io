# A22 — Performance VARIANCE (high stddev): sources + low-risk mitigations

Scope: find why Kirito's runtime is *unstable* (high CV), not why it is slow. Probe binary:
`release/ki-linux-x64` (-O2, pool active). Benchmarks: `tools/tests/bench/compare_bench.ki`.
Probes + scripts under `scratchpad/probe/A22/`. **Analysis only — no source edits.** Status: done.

## TL;DR
- **The dominant variance source is the garbage collector's FIXED 100 000-allocation trigger**
  (`vm.hpp:59`, `gcThreshold_ = 100000` at `vm.hpp:319`) driving a **stop-the-world, O(arena-capacity)
  mark-sweep** (`collectGarbage`, `vm.hpp:127`). Total GC cost is ~25–31 % of runtime **and is
  roughly threshold-invariant** (amortizes fine), but it is delivered in **periodic ~0.7–1.0 ms
  lumps** every 100 k allocations. Those lumps are the variance.
- The relative spike is **worst for cheap/tight operations**: on a small-per-rep loop a single
  collection is up to **20–60× the steady per-rep time** (string_ops max/med 20.5×; sum_loop N=1000
  max/med 62.6×). This is exactly where users perceive "unstable."
- Removing the GC spikes collapses intra-run CV by **3–6×** (e.g. sum_loop 0.590 → 0.120,
  string_ops 1.332 → 0.228, dict_ops 0.645 → 0.165).
- Workloads that delegate to C++ builtins and allocate little per rep (`sort`) show **zero spikes**
  and CV 0.075 — direct proof that allocation volume → GC frequency → variance.

## 2. Variance sources, with evidence

### S1 — GC: fixed 100 k-alloc trigger + O(arena) stop-the-world sweep  **(dominant)**
- **Where:** `vm.hpp:59` `if (gcEnabled_ && ++allocsSinceGc_ >= gcThreshold_) collectGarbage();`
  with `gcThreshold_ = 100000` (`vm.hpp:319`), never adaptive. `collectGarbage` (`vm.hpp:127`) does
  `arena_.clearMarks()` (O(capacity), `arena.hpp:41`), a mark from all roots (O(live)), and
  `arena_.sweep()` (O(capacity), `arena.hpp:54`) — **two full linear scans of the entire `slots_`
  vector** every collection, plus a fresh `std::vector<Handle> work`/`childbuf` per call.
- **The CLI never tunes it** (grep of `main.cpp`/`cli_paths.hpp`: no `setGcThreshold`/`setGcEnabled`/
  env override — only `HOME`/`KIRITO_PATH`). Every real `ki` run gets the fixed 100 k policy. GC
  tuning exists only in unit tests.
- **Evidence — spikes are periodic and match the 100 k arithmetic.** Spike spacing (reps between
  collections) × (allocations/rep) ≈ 100 000 across every workload:
  - sum_loop N=2000: spike every ~13 reps → ~7.7 k allocs/rep (loop `s=s+i*2-i`: i,i*2,product,sum ≈ 4 boxes × 2000).
  - dict_ops N=2000: every ~25 reps → ~4 k allocs/rep (`k*k` + node × 2000).
  - string_ops N=2000: every ~50 reps → ~2 k allocs/rep (2000 split words).
  - sieve N=5000: every ~4–13 reps (variable working set).
  - **sort N=2000: NO spikes** — `base.copy()`+builtin `sort` reuse existing int handles, few *new*
    boxes/rep, so 400 reps never reach 100 k → CV 0.075. This is the control that pins the cause.
- **Evidence — total GC cost is threshold-invariant but lumpy.** Sweeping N for sum_loop
  (`hist.py`): GC's share of runtime stays **~28 % (31.0 / 25.4 / 29.0 %)** at N=1000/2000/4000
  while spacing tracks alloc volume — i.e. the collector amortizes correctly overall; the *problem is
  the distribution*. Per-collection **net pause ≈ 0.7–1.0 ms** (975 k / 769 k / 681 k ns after
  subtracting steady per-rep), fairly flat because it is dominated by the two O(capacity) scans of the
  ~100 k-slot arena, not by the (small) live set.
- **Evidence — worst relative impact on cheap ops.** At N=1000 (steady rep ~135 µs) a 0.98 ms pause
  is **max/med 62.6×**; at N=4000 (steady rep ~570 µs) the same absolute pause is only 5.0×. The
  absolute pause is ~constant; its *relative* damage explodes as per-op work shrinks — so tight loops,
  short scripts, and micro-benchmarks are the most unstable, which is precisely the user's complaint.
- **Why the threshold is the lever, formally:** capacity ≈ threshold + peak-live (up to `threshold`
  dead-but-unswept boxes accumulate between collections). Total GC work = (allocs/threshold) ×
  O(capacity) ≈ O(allocs) — **independent of threshold**. So lowering the threshold shrinks each
  pause and the arena high-water *proportionally*, raises frequency proportionally, and leaves total
  cost ~unchanged: a **near-free variance reduction** down to the floor where re-marking the fixed
  baseline (builtins/stdlib live set, well under ~20 k here) starts to dominate.

### S2 — Pool + arena never shrink → RSS ratchet, page-fault jitter (secondary, cross-refs A08)
- `pool.hpp:40` free-lists only grow (A08-3); `arena.hpp` `slots_` only grows to high-water. A burst
  inflates capacity permanently, so **every subsequent GC pays the inflated O(capacity) scan** even
  after the burst — a slow spike that never recovers, and larger RSS → more major page faults under
  memory pressure (adds tail latency). Not the primary cause here (steady workloads), but it
  compounds S1: the arena the collector must scan is the all-time max, not the current need.

### S3 — Per-call operand-stack heap alloc (minor, call-heavy code)
- `bytecode_vm.hpp:50` every Kirito call constructs a `BytecodeVM` with a fresh `stack_.reserve(16)`
  → one malloc/free (128 B) per call, outside the arena/pool (goes to glibc). For fib (~243 k calls)
  this is steady malloc churn; contributes to fib's moderate CV (0.061) via allocator jitter, not
  spikes. `std::vector` is not covered by the small-object pool.

### S4 — Small-int interning misses on loop counters (minor, amplifies S1)
- `kSmallIntLo/Hi = -256..256` (`vm.hpp:312-313`). Loop indices and results in these benchmarks
  (N=2000–5000, keys up to 2³¹) fall outside the range, so **every arithmetic result boxes a fresh
  IntVal** → this is what *feeds* the 100 k counter. Doesn't cause variance directly but sets the GC
  cadence. Widening helps only up to the range; loops past it still box.

### S5 — Examined, NOT a significant variance source
- **Dict/Set rehash:** `collections.hpp` `fum::unordered_map` bucket growth reallocates C++ heap, not
  arena — it does **not** feed `allocsSinceGc_`, and its cost is *deterministic per rep* (same every
  rep) → adds to mean, not cross-rep variance. dict_ops' high CV is S1 (spikes every 25 reps), not
  rehash. Constructor-side `reserve` on known sizes exists in a few spots (`collections.hpp:159/167/
  269`, `bytecode_vm.hpp:234`) but the per-rep dict/set build does not pre-size.
- **First-call lazy Proto compile** (`vm.hpp:119-124`): a one-time warmup cost; the bench's warmup
  `once()` excludes it. Adds startup variance only for fresh short scripts (each function compiled on
  first call) — real but small next to S1.

## 3. Ranked LOW-RISK, HIGH-REWARD mitigations (variance-first)

### M1 — Make the GC threshold ADAPTIVE (grow proportional to live set) with a modest floor  ★ top pick
- **What:** after each `collectGarbage`, set `gcThreshold_ = max(kFloor, growth × arena_.liveCount())`
  (e.g. `kFloor ≈ 20000`, `growth ≈ 4`). One-line change at the end of `collectGarbage` +
  initializing `gcThreshold_` to `kFloor`. Keep `setGcThreshold` as a manual override.
- **Why it cuts VARIANCE (not just mean):** collections become **evenly spaced relative to real live
  growth** and, because live set here is tiny and stable, the threshold settles near the floor →
  arena high-water and per-pause cost drop ~5× (100 k → ~20 k slots scanned), so each pause is ~5×
  smaller and ~5× more frequent — same total work, **~5× lower spike magnitude**, i.e. the raw CVs in
  §1b move toward the steady-only column (sum_loop 0.590→~0.15, string_ops 1.33→~0.30). For programs
  with a genuinely large live set the threshold grows with it, avoiding thrashing (collect-every-alloc
  when live is huge) — the property the current fixed constant lacks in both directions.
- **Risk:** **Low.** Changes only *when* GC runs; correctness/reachability untouched (same
  mark-sweep). Existing GC unit tests set explicit thresholds and are unaffected. Only behavioural
  risk: floor too low would raise mean by re-marking the baseline too often — mitigated by choosing
  `kFloor` comfortably above the stdlib live set (measured pause floor is O(capacity)-dominated, so
  baseline mark is small).
- **Expected effect:** raw CV of allocation-heavy workloads → roughly the steady-only CV (3–6× lower);
  max/med tail from ~6–60× down to ~1.5–3×. Mean ~unchanged (GC total is threshold-invariant, §2 S1).
- **Backing:** §2 S1 threshold-invariance measurement + §1b steady-only columns.

### M2 — Lower the default fixed threshold (simplest possible variant of M1)  ★ if M1 is deemed too much
- **What:** change `gcThreshold_ = 100000` → e.g. `20000` (`vm.hpp:319`). Nothing else.
- **Why it cuts variance:** identical mechanism to M1 (smaller arena high-water, smaller/more-frequent
  pauses, threshold-invariant total). A one-constant change with the same ~5× spike-magnitude
  reduction, minus M1's self-scaling for large-live programs.
- **Risk:** **Very low** (single constant). Downside vs M1: a program with a large steady live set
  collects a bit more often than optimal (fixed, not proportional) — a mild mean cost, no correctness
  effect.
- **Expected effect:** same tail reduction as M1 for the measured workloads; slightly worse than M1
  for large-heap programs.
- **Backing:** same as M1.

### M3 — Cap pool free-list length + bound arena high-water reuse (variance floor + RSS)
- **What:** cap each `pool.hpp` size-class free-list (e.g. a few thousand blocks) and
  `::operator delete` the overflow (A08-3 fix (c)); optionally shrink `arena_` capacity accounting so
  the O(capacity) scan tracks *current* need not all-time peak.
- **Why it cuts variance:** removes the "one burst permanently inflates every future GC pause + RSS"
  ratchet (§2 S2) → prevents a slow, non-recovering rise in pause cost and reduces major-page-fault
  tail latency on long-running processes.
- **Risk:** **Low–Medium** (touches the hot allocator; needs the size-class boundary care A08 already
  documented + a release-mode RSS test since sanitizers bypass the pool).
- **Expected effect:** stabilizes pause cost across bursty phases; bounds RSS. Secondary to M1/M2 for
  the steady benchmarks but important for real long-lived embeds/servers.
- **Backing:** A08-3; §2 S2.

### M4 — Reserve per-rep container capacity where the final size is known (mean + micro-variance)
- **What:** pre-`reserve` the operand stack for known `localCount`+max depth, and pre-size dict/set/
  list builds when the count is known (e.g. `copy`, `split`, known-length loops).
- **Why it helps variance:** eliminates the reallocate-at-unlucky-size steps whose timing depends on
  when growth boundaries fall — small, but removes a class of per-op micro-jitter (S3/S5).
- **Risk:** **Low.** Pure sizing hints, no semantic change.
- **Expected effect:** minor CV improvement; mostly a mean win. Do after M1/M2.

### M5 — Widen small-int interning (mean, mild variance via lower GC cadence)
- **What:** raise `kSmallIntHi`/lower `kSmallIntLo` (e.g. ±1024 or ±4096).
- **Why:** fewer boxed IntVals for small-counter loops → slower approach to the GC threshold → fewer
  collections in a given run. Cuts the *number* of spikes (not their size), so it trims both mean and
  variance for loops within the range.
- **Risk:** **Low** (more startup memory: ±4096 = ~8 k pre-boxed ints, trivial). No correctness risk.
- **Expected effect:** modest; loops past the range (N≥the bound, the benchmark case) still box. Best
  as a cheap complement to M1/M2.

### Recommended sequencing
1. **M1** (adaptive threshold) — the single highest variance-per-risk win; or **M2** if a one-constant
   change is preferred for this release.
2. **M3** (pool/arena cap) — removes the ratchet so M1/M2's gains hold on long-running processes.
3. **M4/M5** — cheap mean+micro-variance polish afterward.

All five are behaviour-preserving (no change to reachability, value semantics, or the mark-sweep
correctness); M1/M2 alone should move the allocation-heavy workloads from CV 0.6–1.3 down toward the
0.12–0.28 steady-state band.
